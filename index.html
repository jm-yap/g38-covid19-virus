Hi. We are Frontline.

In the age of information, disinformation poses a significant challenge. Social media platforms have become breeding grounds for misleading news, causing numerous users to fall victim to false narratives. The COVID-19 pandemic, which confined people to their homes and increased their reliance on phones, has made Filipinos more susceptible to fake news and even led to skepticism about the virus itself. This study, titled "Scamdemic lang yan! A Content Analysis on Filipino Tweets Spreading False Claims About the COVID-19 Virus," aims to investigate specific false claims regarding the existence of COVID-19.

Motivation

Twitter has become a breeding ground for misinformation causing Filipinos to fall victim to false claims about the COVID-19 virus.

Problem

People are spreading mis/disinformation that the COVID-19 virus is not real.

Research Question

Are there more prevalent false claims about COVID-19 that contribute to Filipinos' disbelief in the virus?

Null Hypothesis

The false claims about COVID-19 are equally likely to contribute to Filipinos' disbelief in the virus.

Alternative Hypothesis

Certain false claims about COVID-19 have a greater likelihood of contributing to Filipinos' disbelief in the virus compared to others.

Action Plan

Analyze the frequency of tweets about false claims that contribute to Filipinos' belief that the COVID-19 virus is not real.

Statistical Test

Chi Square Test

As we are dealing with the frequency of categorical data, the group implemented the Chi Square test in checking the distribution of each category. From the null hypothesis, we expect that tweets that do not beleive in the COVID-19 virus use various false claims in equal proportion. Therefore, the expected value for each is 150/6 = 25.

Chi Squared Values

The observed and expected frequencies were encoded in the given table below. Using the observed frequencies of each category, we computed the mean and standard deviation using Google Sheet's built-in tools. From there, we manually calculated the Chi Squared values of each category to obtain the sum.

Test for Significance

To test for significance in the data distribution, we performed a goodness-of-fit test in the critical region at a 0.05 significance level and 5 degrees of freedom. With the help of Google Sheet's built-in Right Tail Chi Square Distribution function, we found that the p-value for our dataset is 7.46E-05, which is considerably less than the significance level. Hence, we reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternative hypothesis that states that the categorical data are not in equal proportion.

Standardizing Residuals

The standarized residuals per category were computed through the difference of the observed and expected value, divided by the square root of the expected value. This operation was performed on the sheet. Upon checking the standardized scores, it was found that the false claim of COVID-19 being a financial scheme of private sectors is a major contributor to the disbelief in the virus according to the tweets as it obtained a score greater than +2. On the other hand, the false claim on it being a fear tactic was determined as a weak contributor with a standardized score of less than -2.

Machine Learning

Latent Dirichlet Allocation

Topic clustering through Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) was used to categorize tweets in the dataset. Associated keywords were collected to form a list of categories to automatically label each tweet through NLP. LDA is a generative probabilistic model under unsupervised machine learning. It assumes that each tweet is composed by each topic and computes the probability of a word belonging to them. As the tweets were tokenized into keywords through preprocessing (see Data Exploration), the authors should initialize the specific parameters of the model. These variables include the number of topics to be generated and the number of keywords to represent each topic. With that, LDA can now analyze their distribution of words through the following steps:

For each word in each tweet,

  1. randomly assign them to a topic
  2. compute its probability to belong in each topic based on the current document
  3. compute its probability to belong in each topic based on the whole sample
  4. update the probability of each word belonging to each topic
  5. resample the topic assignment for each word based on its probability
  6. repeat the from step 2 until the model converges or reaches a specified number of iterations

In order to come up with coherent key words for each topic, stop words that were not in the package in the preprocessing were excluded or included. While removing stop words is used to reduce noise and improve efficiency, there are situations where their inclusion can provide advantages in terms of information retrieval.

Starting with a small number of topics, LDA was executed multiple times. Manual inspection was done until meaningful and distinct topics were obtained.

Through LDA, 4 topics were generated namely,

  1. Financial Scheme - entails perpetuating the pandemic to profit from it financially
    Keywords: money, stupid, fake, devil, believe, real, corrupt, business, increase, let
  2. Government Fabrication - suggests that the virus itself does not exist and is just a deliberate fabrication orchestrated by the government
    Keywords: economy, dutertepalpak, research, government, fake, scam, country, money, making, pneumonia
  3. Conspiracy Theory - suggests hidden agendas and secret plots, such as the pandemic is tool for control or manipulation, by influential people or groups
    Keywords: omicron, china, image, vaccinated, chinese, deadly, communist, going, research, brain
  4. Others - any claims about the COVID-19 virus that doesn't belong to the categories mentioned above (e.g. misclassified illness, lack of personal experience)
    Keywords: medicine, world, variant, name, hoax, disease, isolated, whole, kill, time

Data Communication

Introduction

Since 2019, the COVID-19 pandemic has greatly affected the economy, livelihood, and overall well-being of several countries. Various measures have been taken to minimize its impact, such as proper health protocols, mass testing, and vaccination. However, with the rise of false information regarding the virus, citizens may be prone to vaccine hesitancy and disbelief in COVID-19. To spread awareness regarding misinformation regarding the COVID-19 virus being shared on social media (i.e. Twitter), this study analyzes the common false claims associated with Filipinos‚Äô disbelief in the virus

Materials and Methods

  1. Data Collection
    150 tweets (within the years 2020-2022) from Filipino users claiming that COVID-19 is not real were scraped from Twitter using associated keywords and the following techniques:
    • Twitter Scrapers (Apify, snscrape)
    • Manual Searching (Twitter advanced search)
  2. Preprocessing
    The authors applied Natural Language Processing through Python packages to convert the collected data into tokens
    • Handling missing values
    • Formatting consistency
    • Lowercasing and translation, tokenization
    • Stop words removal
    • Stemming and lemmatization
  3. Data Modeling
    Topic clustering through Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) was used to categorize tweets in the dataset. Associated keywords were collected to form a list of categories to automatically label each tweet through NLP.

  4. Statistical Test
    The Chi-square goodness of fit test at a 0.05 significance level was utilized to assess if the frequency distribution of each category among the dataset is equal.

Results and Discussion

The research findings suggest an unequal distribution of the frequency of false claims that contributes to Filipinos‚Äô skepticism about the existence of the COVID-19 virus. Despite the persistence of numerous false claims on this topic, the research identified distinct categories of false claims with varying frequencies.

However, the research is limited in that it does not establish a direct link between the frequency of false claims and their influence. Further investigations are required to explore this aspect. Moreover, the development of a model capable of identifying the specific false claim/s responsible for such an unequal association to the disbelief would be highly beneficial.

This study and its ongoing continuation hold significant value due to their potential to mitigate or even eradicate the adverse effects of disinformation across various domains, extending beyond the context of COVID-19. Our findings add to people's existing knowledge about misinformation and its role in public perception. Therefore, by identifying and debunking the false claim most strongly associated with the topic, we can effectively reduce the larger number of people who believe in the piece of disinformation.

Implications

The study offers a promising strategy to mitigate the impact of disinformation, as highlighted in the results. By identifying and debunking the most influential false claim contributing to misguided beliefs, we can effectively reduce the number of individuals affected by it.

Moreover, the model developed in this study holds practical value beyond its current application. It can be utilized in other research endeavors aiming to categorize large datasets into specific categories. Additionally, the model provides statistical tests that enable comprehensive analysis of these categories, leading to conclusive results.

Conclusion

Based on the study's findings, the false claims do not have an equal distribution in the sample data collected. Hence, there may be underlying factors that influence the prevalence of COVID-19 disbelief with regards to the aforementioned false claims. The results would be beneficial in extending the research involving the correlation between COVID-19 denial and particular false claims. The results of further research can contribute in planning initiatives for educating the public about more prevalent false claims that are associated with COVID-19 disbelief.
